Option 1 — "The Reading": Narrative, cinematic, watching Sarah open her blueprint
What Comes Out

She opened this document at 11pm on a Tuesday.
She didn't close it until 2am.

Sarah Chen spent six years building a coaching practice. She could transform a client's career in three months. She could not explain what she did in three sentences. This is the document that changed that.

Brand Blueprint — Sarah Chen — Deep Work Interview Output
She read this line four times. Then she sent it to her business partner with no context. His reply: "Where has this been?"
"Sarah Chen is the only leadership coach who uses 12 years at McKinsey to help burned-out executives redesign their careers without sacrificing income or identity."
One conversation. That's all it took. The words were already inside her. She just needed someone to find them.
This is the part that made her cry.
Marcus, 44. SVP at a tech company. At 11pm on a Tuesday he's scrolling LinkedIn watching people ten years younger get promoted around him. His wife knows something's off. He tells people he's "evaluating options." He's too proud to admit he doesn't know what comes next. He has never paid less than $500/hr for advice. He's been waiting for someone like you.
She had never described her ideal client like this. She had never needed to. After reading it, she couldn't unsee him. He was already in her inbox. She just hadn't known how to speak to him.
This is the line she put on her website the next morning.
"You built a career everyone admires. Now you need someone who'll tell you the truth about what comes next."
Five headline options. Five different emotional angles. She picked this one because it sounded like something she would actually say — except sharper, braver, and more honest than she'd ever allowed herself to be.
Every word in this document came from what Sarah said during the conversation. Nothing was invented. Nothing was templated.
Your version will be different. Your story is different. But the feeling — the feeling of finally reading words that sound like the version of you that's been waiting to exist — that part is the same.
